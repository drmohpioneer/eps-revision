E5 - Ventricular Tachycardia (VT) and ARVC

Three mechanisms, the idiopathic-VT localizer (RVOT vs fascicular), and the ARVC panel. Exam anchors: Q9 (RVOT), Q22 (digoxin = delayed afterdepolarization), fascicular = reentry.

1. The three arrhythmia mechanisms (click)

Reentry = a self-sustaining circuit

The impulse never dies; it keeps circling a loop and re-enters tissue it already fired. Needs two paths, unidirectional block in one, slow conduction in the other. The loop sits around a fixed obstacle, classically an old MI scar (muscle strands weaving through dead scar = the slow zone).

VTs that use it: most sustained monomorphic VT, scar-related (post-MI) VT, and fascicular VT (verapamil-sensitive). Reentry can often be paced-terminated / entrained.
Enhanced (abnormal) automaticity = a focus firing too fast

A patch of ventricle spontaneously depolarizes faster than the sinus node and steals the rhythm. Driven by catecholamines, acute ischaemia, electrolyte shifts. No circuit, so it usually cannot be started or stopped by a pacing beat (it just warms up and cools down).

VTs that use it: accelerated idioventricular rhythm, some catecholamine-driven / ischaemic ventricular ectopy.
Triggered activity - EAD (early afterdepolarization)

An abnormal depolarization rises on the tail of the last beat before repolarization finishes (phase 2/3, while the QT is still long). Favoured by bradycardia, hypokalaemia, long QT, QT-prolonging drugs.

VT it causes: torsades de pointes (polymorphic, twisting). This is E6.
Triggered activity - DAD (delayed afterdepolarization)

An abnormal depolarization fires after repolarization is complete (phase 4), driven by intracellular calcium overload. Favoured by digoxin toxicity, catecholamines, fast rates.

Q22 nuance: digoxin toxicity -> calcium overload -> DAD -> triggered beat. On the MCQ, "triggered activity" and "late afterdepolarization" are the same mechanism; the key picks D (the afterdepolarization is the actual trigger).
VTs it causes: digoxin-induced VT (often bidirectional), CPVT, and RVOT (cAMP-mediated) VT.

  • Origin: RV outflow tract, just under the pulmonary valve.
  • Mechanism: triggered activity (cAMP-mediated DADs), so catecholamine-sensitive (exercise, stress, caffeine).
  • ECG: LBBB morphology (leaves the RV -> negative V1) + inferior axis (fires from the top downward -> tall positive R in II/III/aVF).
  • Prognosis: good, structurally normal heart.
  • Treatment: beta-blockers, verapamil, adenosine; ablation is curative.

  • Origin: left fascicular / Purkinje network (usually left posterior fascicle).
  • Mechanism: reentry (the recall) -> verapamil-sensitive, can be paced-terminated.
  • ECG: relatively narrow-ish RBBB pattern + LEFT-axis (posterior-fascicle exit heads up-and-left).
  • Who: young patients, structurally normal heart.
  • Treatment: IV verapamil breaks it; ablation for recurrence.
Mirror rule: RVOT = LBBB + inferior + triggered + adenosine/beta-blocker. Fascicular = RBBB + left-axis + reentry + verapamil. They are opposite on every axis. Scar VT (structural heart) is also reentry but needs an ICD.

4. ARVC - arrhythmogenic RIGHT ventricular cardiomyopathy

The name is arrhythmogenic RV cardiomyopathy, NOT "dysplasia". Fibrofatty replacement of RV muscle (especially RVOT + moderator band); may also involve the LV.
Presents as exercise-induced syncope / VT (can also fire at rest); a cause of sudden death in young athletes.
Its VT arises in the RV, so it has LBBB morphology (leaves the RV heading left). Contrast RVOT VT: benign, normal heart.
Resting ECG epsilon wave (terminal notch after QRS) + T-wave inversion in right precordial leads V1 to V3.
Echo / MRI dilated RV, impaired RV systolic function, thinned / dyskinetic wall; MRI shows fibrofatty replacement.
