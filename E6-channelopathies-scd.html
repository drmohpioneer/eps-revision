E6 - Channelopathies and Sudden Cardiac Death (SCD)

Inherited arrhythmia syndromes, the QT interval and its afterdepolarizations, and who gets an ICD. Exam anchors: Q10 (torsades cause EXCEPT = hypercalcaemia), Q14 (which is NOT high-risk SCD), Q15 (first Brugada gene = SCN5A), Q23 (resuscitated young patient = ICD + beta-blocker), plus LQT2 = deactivated K channel and flecainide does NOT shorten QT.

1. The channelopathy selector (click one)

Six inherited syndromes. Match each to its gene, its trigger, its ECG, and its treatment. The pattern of the exam is: name the gene, name the trigger, name the therapy.

Long QT type 1 Loss of K current (IKs)
Gene / channelKCNQ1, loss of function of the slow delayed-rectifier K current (IKs). The most common LQTS.
TriggerExercise, classically swimming / diving. Adrenergic.
Resting ECGLong QT with a broad-based, prolonged T wave.
TreatmentBeta-blockers are highly protective (the best-responding subtype). Avoid QT drugs. ICD for secondary prevention or high risk.
Mechanism of every long QT event: the prolonged repolarization lets an early afterdepolarization (EAD) fire, which starts torsades de pointes. See section 2.
Long QT type 2 Loss of K current (IKr)
Gene / channelKCNH2 (HERG), loss of function of the rapid delayed-rectifier K current (IKr). This is the recall: LQT2 mechanism = a deactivated / loss-of-function K channel.
TriggerEmotion and sudden auditory startle (alarm clock, ringing phone, doorbell).
Resting ECGLong QT with a low-amplitude, notched / bifid T wave.
TreatmentBeta-blockers, avoid QT-prolonging drugs, keep potassium high, remove startle triggers. ICD if high risk.
Exam recall: "Long QT type 2 mechanism = deactivated K channel" means the IKr (HERG / KCNH2) potassium channel is lost / non-functioning, so repolarizing K current falls and the QT lengthens. Same drug channel that acquired (drug-induced) long QT blocks.
Long QT type 3 Gain of Na current (late)
Gene / channelSCN5A, gain of function of the cardiac sodium channel, a persistent late sodium current that keeps the cell depolarized.
TriggerEvents at rest and during sleep (bradycardia-dependent), not exercise.
Resting ECGLong isoelectric ST segment then a late-appearing peaked T wave.
TreatmentBeta-blockers are less protective here; mexiletine (a late-Na blocker) shortens the QT. ICD for high risk.
Same gene, opposite direction: SCN5A loss of function = Brugada (and LQT3 is SCN5A gain). One gene, two syndromes.
Brugada syndrome Loss of Na current (SCN5A)
Gene / channelSCN5A, loss of function of the cardiac sodium channel. It is the first and most common gene identified (Q15). Autosomal dominant, male predominance.
TriggerVF and SCD at rest, during sleep, and provoked by fever (and vagal tone, alcohol, certain drugs).
Resting ECGCoved ST elevation in V1 to V2 (type 1), with an RBBB-like pattern. Structurally normal heart. See section 4.
TreatmentICD is the only proven therapy. Quinidine as an adjunct (reduces VF episodes). Aggressively treat fever, avoid triggering drugs.
Short QT syndrome Gain of K current
Gene / channelGain of function of K channels (KCNH2, KCNQ1, KCNJ2), so repolarization finishes too early and the QT is very short.
PresentationAF, syncope, and SCD in young patients. Very short QT (roughly under 330 to 360 ms).
Resting ECGShort QT with tall, peaked, narrow T waves and a near-absent ST segment.
TreatmentICD for high risk. Quinidine lengthens the QT back toward normal.
Long vs short is a mirror: long QT = loss of K current (IKs / IKr). Short QT = gain of K current. See the short QT tracing in section 3.
CPVT (catecholaminergic polymorphic VT) Calcium leak (RyR2)
Gene / channelRyR2 (ryanodine receptor) or CASQ2 (calsequestrin). A diastolic calcium leak from the sarcoplasmic reticulum drives delayed afterdepolarizations (DADs).
TriggerCatecholamines: exercise and emotion. Presents as exertional syncope or arrest in a young person.
Resting ECGNormal at rest. On exertion it produces bidirectional or polymorphic VT.
TreatmentBeta-blockers (nadolol) first line, add flecainide, avoid competitive sport, ICD for survivors, left cardiac sympathetic denervation if refractory.
This fits Q23: a young resuscitated patient with a family history of SCD, syncope, and a normal or borderline structural heart is the classic inherited-arrhythmia arrest (CPVT or long QT). The answer is ICD (secondary prevention) plus beta-blockers.

2. The QT interval, EADs, and torsades

The QT interval is the whole action potential (depolarization to full repolarization). A long QT means repolarization drags. That slow tail is where an early afterdepolarization (EAD) can rise and re-fire the cell before it has finished resetting, launching torsades.

Chain to memorize: long QT (any cause) -> slow repolarization -> EAD (early afterdepolarization, phase 2 or 3) -> triggered beat -> torsades de pointes -> can degenerate to VF and SCD. (Contrast: DAD = delayed afterdepolarization, calcium overload, drives digoxin VT and CPVT. See E5.)

3. What lengthens vs what shortens the QT (Q10 and the flecainide recall)

Prolong QT -> torsades risk
  • Drugs: macrolides (erythromycin, azithromycin, clarithromycin), typical antipsychotics (haloperidol), class Ia (quinidine, procainamide) and class III (sotalol, amiodarone) antiarrhythmics, antiemetics (ondansetron), methadone, some antifungals, TCAs.
  • Electrolytes: hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia, hypocalcaemia.
  • Rate / other: bradycardia and heart block, hypothyroidism, hypothermia, raised intracranial pressure, congenital LQTS.
Shorten QT
  • Hypercalcaemia (high calcium shortens the ST and the QT).
  • Hyperkalaemia.
  • Digoxin (the "reverse tick" and short QT).
  • Acidosis, catecholamines / sympathetic tone, fever and hyperthermia.
  • Congenital short QT syndrome (K-channel gain).
Flecainide does NOT shorten the QT (recall Q19). Flecainide is a class Ic sodium blocker; it slows conduction and widens the QRS and can even prolong the QT/QTc a little. It is not a QT-shortening drug.
Q10, the trap: "All of the following cause torsades EXCEPT" -> the answer is hypercalcaemia. Everything that causes torsades does so by prolonging the QT (hypokalaemia, macrolides, typical antipsychotics all prolong). Hypercalcaemia SHORTENS the QT, so it cannot cause torsades. Read the flag: hypocalcaemia prolongs, hypercalcaemia shortens.
Short QT tracing removed. The image previously here was mislabeled (it is actually a long QT with PVCs). For reference, a true short QT shows tall, narrow, peaked T waves sitting almost on the QRS with a near-absent ST segment and a QTc under about 330 to 360 ms. A verified short-QT ECG will be added.
Early repolarization (recall Q7): a J wave / J-point elevation and slurring or notching at the QRS-ST junction, classically in the inferolateral leads (II, III, aVF and the lateral chest leads), not confined to the chest leads. Usually a benign normal variant; a malignant form (inferior J waves with a horizontal ST) links to idiopathic VF. The wrong statement to spot is "the J wave is in the chest leads", because early-repolarization J waves are classically inferolateral, not chest-only.

4. Brugada syndrome: the coved ST (Q15)

SCN5A loss of function (the first and most common gene, Q15). A structurally normal heart with a hidden risk of VF at rest, in sleep, or with fever. Male predominance. The ICD is the only proven therapy.

Key facts: gene = SCN5A (loss of function, first identified). Type 1 = coved ST >=2 mm in V1 to V2. Events at rest, in sleep, and with fever (treat fever fast). ICD is the only proven therapy; quinidine is an adjunct. Avoid triggering drugs.

5. HCM Risk-SCD and who gets an ICD

In hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, two questions: has the patient already had a life-threatening event, or are we predicting risk?

Secondary prevention (already happened) = Class I ICD: a survivor of cardiac arrest due to VF or a haemodynamically unstable VT. No score needed; implant.

Otherwise, estimate the 5-year SCD risk with the HCM Risk-SCD score. The seven factors:

1Maximal LV wall thickness
2Left atrial diameter
3Maximal LVOT gradient
4Age
5Non-sustained VT (NSVT)
6Unexplained syncope
7Family history of SCD
SituationICD class
Survivor of VF arrest or haemodynamically unstable VTClass I
5-year risk >=6%Class IIa
5-year risk 4 to <6%Class IIb
5-year risk <4%Class IIb only if other risk modifiers
Q14 anchor: a HOCM risk score of 8 (that is a 5-year risk of 8%, well above 6%) is a genuinely high-risk patient (IIa or more). So is QTc >500 with a family history of SCD, and a prior MI with EF 25% and recurrent VT (secondary-prevention ICD). What is NOT a validated high-risk marker is frequent PVCs induced during a stress ECG in an ischaemic patient: that is the answer to Q14 (the one that is NOT high risk).

6. High-risk SCD, at a glance (Q14 and Q23)

Genuinely high risk
  • Yes Survivor of VF arrest or unstable VT (secondary prevention, Class I).
  • Yes QTc >500 ms plus family history of SCD.
  • Yes Prior MI, EF 25%, recurrent VT.
  • Yes HCM Risk-SCD score high (for example 8, i.e. >=6%).
NOT a validated high-risk marker
  • No Frequent PVCs provoked on a stress ECG in an ischaemic patient. Common and non-specific; not a validated SCD stratifier (Q14 answer).
Q23 pattern: young woman, family history of SCD, syncope, resuscitated (aborted SCD), abnormal ECG = an inherited arrhythmia syndrome (long QT or CPVT). Because she already arrested, this is secondary prevention: implant a defibrillator and give beta-blockers.
