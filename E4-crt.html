Cardiac Resynchronization Therapy (CRT)

A normal pacemaker fixes a heart that is too slow. CRT fixes a failing heart that beats out of sync. In LBBB the lateral LV wall is activated last; CRT adds a third lead on that wall and fires it early, so both walls squeeze together and the wide QRS narrows. Everything below is interactive.

Rate vs timing — the whole idea

Normal pacemaker (E3)
Fixes RATE
Heart is too slow. Kick a chamber so it does not pause.
Problem = bradycardia.
CRT
Fixes TIMING
Rate is fine, but LBBB makes the LV walls contract out of step. Pace both ventricles together to resynchronize.
Problem = dyssynchrony in a failing LV.
In LBBB the impulse crawls muscle to muscle to the lateral / posterolateral LV wall, which arrives last. That late wall is exactly where the LV lead goes.

Wide LBBB QRS narrows with CRT — press pace

Firing the late lateral wall early, at the same time as the RV, lets the two wavefronts fuse in the middle instead of one chasing the other. Total activation time (QRS width) drops.
Paced-ECG signature (the "ECG in CRT" recall): the LV lead paces the lateral wall, so depolarization now runs left to right. Look for a dominant R in V1 (RBBB-like) and a negative QRS in lead I. Lose that pattern and you have lost LV capture (RV-only pacing).

Indication checker — set the patient, see if CRT is indicated

Gates (ESC): LVEF <= 35%, sinus rhythm, on optimal medical therapy first. QRS: >=150 ms = strongest, 130-149 ms = weaker, <130 ms = not indicated (can harm). LBBB is the strong substrate; non-LBBB benefits far less.
