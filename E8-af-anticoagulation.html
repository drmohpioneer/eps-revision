E8 - Atrial Fibrillation and Anticoagulation

A fibrillating atrium throws clots. Score the stroke risk against the bleed risk, pick and dose the anticoagulant, time it safely around any stroke, and control the rhythm without a pro-arrhythmic drug. Exam anchors: Q11 (rivaroxaban 20 mg OD), Q12 (propafenone is the EXCEPT in an ischaemic patient), Q18 (restart OAC 1 day after a TIA), Q19 (CHA2DS2-VASc, with the key conflict), Q20 (rtPA not if INR > 1.7), Q21 (apixaban best for GI bleed).

2. The NOACs: which drug, what dose, when to reduce (click one)

Four drugs, two mechanisms. -xaban = Xa ban (apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban block factor Xa); dabigatran blocks thrombin. Default for non-valvular AF. Click a drug for its standard dose and its reduction trigger.

Rivaroxaban Factor Xa inhibitor

Standard AF dose: 20 mg once daily (with food). Reduce to 15 mg once daily if CrCl 15 to 49 mL/min.

This is Q11: the non-valvular AF dose of rivaroxaban is 20 mg once daily (15 mg OD if CrCl 15 to 49). Do not confuse it with the lower VTE-prophylaxis or vascular-dose numbers. Note: rivaroxaban increases GI bleeding, so it is not the first pick for a GI bleeder.
Apixaban Factor Xa inhibitor

Standard AF dose: 5 mg twice daily. Reduce to 2.5 mg twice daily if any 2 of 3: age >= 80, weight <= 60 kg, creatinine >= 1.5 mg/dL.

This is Q21: apixaban has the most favourable GI-bleeding profile of the NOACs, so in a patient at high risk of gastrointestinal bleeding, apixaban 5 mg twice daily is the best choice. (Dabigatran 150 and rivaroxaban both raise GI bleeds.)
Dabigatran Direct thrombin (IIa) inhibitor

Standard AF dose: 150 mg twice daily. Reduce to 110 mg twice daily in the elderly (age >= 80), high bleeding risk, or moderate renal impairment.

The only thrombin inhibitor of the group (the others block factor Xa). Dabigatran 150 mg raises GI bleeding and needs decent renal function (mostly renally cleared). Contraindicated in mechanical valves (it caused harm there).
Edoxaban Factor Xa inhibitor

Standard AF dose: 60 mg once daily. Reduce to 30 mg once daily if CrCl 15 to 50, weight <= 60 kg, or on a strong P-gp inhibitor.

Curiously, edoxaban is not recommended when renal function is too good (very high CrCl), because clearance is too fast for reliable effect.

"Non-valvular" = NOAC eligible. The only exclusions ("valvular AF") are a mechanical prosthetic valve and moderate-to-severe mitral stenosis: those must use warfarin, never a NOAC. Every other valve situation still uses a NOAC.

3. Timing OAC around a stroke: the 1-3-6-12 rule (click a severity)

After an ischaemic brain event the infarct can bleed (haemorrhagic transformation). The bigger the infarct, the longer you wait before (re)starting anticoagulation. Click a severity to see the delay move along the timeline.

TIA: restart OAC after 1 day. No established infarct, so lowest bleeding risk. (This is Q18.)
Two different numbers, do not fuse them: the 1-3-6-12 ladder answers "when do I put the patient back on their long-term anticoagulant?" (TIA 1, mild 3, moderate 6, severe 12 days). The INR 1.7 gate answers a different acute question below.
Thrombolysis gate (Q20): systemic thrombolysis with rtPA / alteplase is NOT recommended if the INR is above 1.7. An already-anticoagulated brain that you thrombolyse bleeds catastrophically. (The same bar applies to a recent DOAC within its exclusion window.)

4. Rhythm vs rate control, and antiarrhythmic drug safety (Q12)

Two strategies. Rate control accepts the AF and slows the ventricle; rhythm control restores sinus rhythm. The drug that maintains sinus rhythm depends on whether the heart is normal or diseased. Click to compare.

Rate control

Accept the AF, slow the ventricular response. Agents:

  • Beta-blockers (first line for most).
  • Non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers: diltiazem, verapamil (avoid in HFrEF).
  • Digoxin: add-on, or in heart failure / sedentary patients.
Rhythm control: structurally NORMAL heart

Maintain sinus rhythm. With a normal heart you can use the class Ic drugs:

  • Flecainide and propafenone (often "pill in the pocket").
  • Sotalol, dronedarone, amiodarone are also options.
Class Ic is safe here precisely because there is no scar or ischaemia to make it pro-arrhythmic.
Rhythm control: ISCHAEMIC or structural disease

Class Ic (flecainide, propafenone) is CONTRAINDICATED (pro-arrhythmic in a scarred/ischaemic ventricle, the CAST-trial lesson). Acceptable agents:

  • OK Amiodarone (safe in structural disease and heart failure).
  • OK Sotalol, dofetilide (watch QT and renal function).
  • OK Dronedarone - but contraindicated in heart failure.
  • NO Flecainide, propafenone (class Ic).
This is Q12: for rhythm control in an ischaemic patient after cardioversion, the acceptable agents are amiodarone, sotalol, and dronedarone; the EXCEPT is propafenone (the only class Ic drug in the list). Answer D.
