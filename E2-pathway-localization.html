Accessory Pathway Localization

The delta wave is the ventricle lit early from the pathway. Each lead's delta is an arrow pointing away from the pathway. Read three leads, find the spot.

The 3-step algorithm (always this order)

Step 1 · SIDE
V1 delta
Up (tall R) = LEFT.
Down (QS/rS) = RIGHT.
"V1 Up = Left."
Note (V1 localization): a positive delta wave / dominant R in V1 (type A) points to a LEFT-sided pathway; a negative delta in V1 (type B) points to a RIGHT-sided pathway.
Step 2 · LEVEL
Inferior II/III/aVF
Negative = POSTERIOR (posteroseptal).
Positive = anterior/superior.
"Inferior Neg = Posterior."
Step 3 · LATERAL
Lead I / aVL
Negative = LEFT LATERAL (free wall).
Positive = septal side.
"Lead I Neg = Left Lateral."

Ablation endpoint: did the concealed pathway die?

A concealed pathway has no delta (it conducts retrograde only), so you cannot watch a delta disappear. You test the one thing it does: retrograde (VA) conduction during ventricular pacing. Toggle before vs after the burn.

Before ablation: every ventricular paced beat (S) conducts back up 1:1 to the atria, so the retrograde pathway is still alive.
The tested sign: for a concealed pathway, VA dissociation during ventricular pacing = the pathway is dead. (A manifest pathway instead shows loss of the delta wave.)

Real WPW ECG — spot the delta

Note (pre-excited AF): a fast, wide, IRREGULAR tachycardia in WPW is atrial fibrillation conducting down the accessory pathway. AVOID all AV-nodal blockers (adenosine, verapamil, diltiazem, digoxin, beta-blockers), they can accelerate the pathway and precipitate VF. Use procainamide or ibutilide, or DC cardiovert.

Note (SPERRI): the shortest pre-excited RR interval in AF (SPERRI) of 250 ms or less marks a high-risk pathway (risk of VF and sudden death).
